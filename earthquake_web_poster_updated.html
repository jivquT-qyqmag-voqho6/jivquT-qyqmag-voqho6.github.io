The Event

What Happened?

On February 6, 2023, a massive earthquake struck southeastern Turkey and northern Syria at 4:17 AM while millions of people were asleep. The ground shook so violently that thousands of buildings collapsed within minutes.

The earthquake started 17.9 km underground near the city of Nurdaƒüƒ± in the Gaziantep province ‚Äî right where three of Earth's massive tectonic plates meet.

Just 11 minutes later, a 6.7 magnitude aftershock struck. Then, nine hours later, an even more powerful 7.5 earthquake hit again, making rescue efforts even more dangerous. Countries as far as Cyprus, Egypt, Israel, and Lebanon all felt the shaking.

More than 50 nations sent rescue teams, dogs, and supplies. The death toll eventually exceeded 50,000 people ‚Äî one of the deadliest earthquakes of the 21st century.

Earth Science
Earth's Major Tectonic Plates

Why Did It Happen Here?

Turkey sits on the Anatolian tectonic plate ‚Äî a giant slab of Earth's crust being slowly squeezed by the Arabian plate pushing from the south.

Two massive cracks in the crust (fault lines) border this plate. The East Anatolian Fault ‚Äî which caused the 2023 earthquake ‚Äî had NOT moved for hundreds of years.

Think of it like bending a stick very, very slowly. Pressure builds for centuries until suddenly ‚Äî SNAP! All that stored energy releases at once as an earthquake.

Scientists were surprised that the rupture traveled along such a large section of the fault, which is why shaking was felt across 11 countries.

Engineering

Why So Many Buildings Collapsed

Example of a pancake collapse ‚Äî floors stacking on each other
Old Construction
Many buildings were built before modern safety codes existed. Old concrete and brick crumble easily during strong shaking ‚Äî they weren't designed to flex and survive.
Poor Building Practices
Even when modern plans existed, buildings were sometimes not actually constructed to match them. After a 1999 earthquake, engineers found widespread non-compliance.
ü•û Pancake Collapse
When vertical walls and columns fail, heavy concrete floors fall straight down and stack on top of each other ‚Äî like pancakes. Anyone trapped inside has little chance.
4 AM Strike
Nearly everyone was asleep indoors. There was no time to escape before buildings fell, which dramatically increased the number of fatalities.
Modern Buildings CAN Survive
Prof. Sasani (Northeastern Univ.) said: if buildings had been built following current codes, only a very small percentage would have collapsed at this magnitude.

Foundations

What Is an Earthquake?

Earthquake (noun)
A sudden, violent shaking of the ground caused by the movement of tectonic plates or volcanic activity beneath Earth's surface. Energy is released in the form of seismic waves that travel outward in all directions.

The Earth's outer layer ‚Äî called the crust ‚Äî is not one solid piece. It is broken into giant sections called tectonic plates that float on a layer of hot, semi-molten rock called the mantle. These plates are always moving, just too slowly for us to feel ‚Äî only a few centimetres per year.

When plates grind, collide, or slip past each other, energy builds up at the boundary. When that energy is released suddenly, the ground shakes. This is an earthquake. The underground point where it starts is called the focus; the point on the surface directly above it is the epicenter.

Earthquakes happen constantly around the world ‚Äî thousands per year ‚Äî but most are so small only instruments can detect them. Large earthquakes like the 2023 Turkey‚ÄìSyria event release enormous energy and cause catastrophic damage.

Root Causes

Why Do Earthquakes Occur?

üß©
Tectonic Plate Movement
The most common cause. Earth's crust is divided into 15 major plates that constantly move. Where they meet ‚Äî called fault lines ‚Äî stress builds up until it suddenly releases as an earthquake. About 90% of all earthquakes happen along these boundaries.
üåã
Volcanic Activity
When magma moves underground or a volcano erupts, it can shake the surrounding rock. Volcanic earthquakes are usually smaller but can be frequent, and they often warn that a volcanic eruption is coming.
‚ö°
Fault Line Ruptures
Plates lock together due to friction but keep pushing. Over centuries, stress builds like a spring being compressed. Eventually the rock snaps along the fault ‚Äî releasing all that energy at once. The East Anatolian Fault had been locked for hundreds of years before 2023.
üíß
Human Activities
Some earthquakes are triggered by humans ‚Äî filling large reservoirs (the weight of the water stresses the rock below), deep mining operations, or injecting fluids underground. These are called induced earthquakes and are usually smaller in magnitude.
üèîÔ∏è
Colliding Plates
When two continental plates collide head-on, neither sinks ‚Äî they crumple and push upward, forming mountain ranges like the Himalayas. This collision process produces powerful earthquakes, sometimes hundreds of kilometres from the actual boundary.
üåä
Subduction Zones
When an ocean plate dives beneath a continental plate (called subduction), the two plates grind past each other at great depth. This produces some of the world's most powerful earthquakes ‚Äî including those that trigger devastating tsunamis.
Measurement

Understanding Magnitude

Magnitude measures how much energy an earthquake releases. The scale is logarithmic ‚Äî meaning each whole number is 10 times stronger than the one before it. A magnitude 7 earthquake releases about 30 times MORE energy than a magnitude 6.

Prevention & Mitigation

How to Prevent Earthquake Damage

We cannot stop earthquakes from happening ‚Äî but we can dramatically reduce the damage and deaths they cause. Prevention is really about preparedness and smart engineering.

üèóÔ∏è
Earthquake-Resistant Buildings
Modern buildings are designed to flex without breaking using steel frames, reinforced concrete, and base isolators (shock absorbers under the foundation).
  • Steel frames that bend instead of snap
  • Flexible foundations that absorb shock
  • Cross-bracing and shear walls for stability
  • Heavy objects secured to walls and floors
üîß
Retrofitting Old Buildings
Existing older buildings can be strengthened (retrofitted) rather than demolished. California has been doing this for schools and hospitals for decades.
  • Adding steel bracing inside walls
  • Reinforcing concrete columns and beams
  • Upgrading connection joints between floors
  • Installing base isolation systems
üì°
Early Warning Systems
Sensors detect the first (slower, less damaging) seismic waves and send alerts seconds before the destructive waves arrive ‚Äî enough time to take cover.
  • Japan's ShakeAlert gives 10‚Äì60 seconds warning
  • Trains and factories auto-stop on alert
  • Emergency broadcasts reach millions instantly
  • Medical equipment pauses automatically
üó∫Ô∏è
Smart Urban Planning
Governments can zone high-risk areas to prevent construction on active fault lines, unstable soil, or tsunami flood zones.
  • Prohibit building directly on fault lines
  • Mandate soil testing before construction
  • Design cities with wide roads for evacuation
  • Locate hospitals and schools in low-risk zones
üë©‚Äçüè´
Education & Drills
Countries like Japan run national earthquake drills every year. Knowing exactly what to do in the first 60 seconds saves lives.
  • School Drop, Cover, Hold On drills
  • Community evacuation route maps
  • Public earthquake awareness campaigns
  • Training emergency response volunteers
üåç
Global Cooperation
Rich countries can help poorer nations build safer buildings and response systems ‚Äî because earthquakes do not respect national borders.
  • Share engineering knowledge and technology
  • Fund retrofitting in vulnerable countries
  • Pre-position rescue teams near high-risk zones
  • International seismic monitoring networks
